The Origin

I heard the problem before I had the authority to fix it.

I spent two years as a financial consultant at TIAA sitting across from clients. I heard their frustration firsthand. A client waiting on funds to cover a mortgage payment. A trade that couldn't execute. A letter that never arrived. The front office team would find a solution - and then hit a wall.

There was no structured handoff to back and middle office teams. Resolution meant knowing the right person to email. That email often went to the wrong team. The right team would eventually get looped in, but by then the client had called back twice. Cases that should have resolved in a day were taking 3 to 5 business days - if they resolved at all.

When I was promoted into a management role, I had a choice: accept the broken process or do something about it. I decided to build a fix.

"The pain was distributed across every layer of the organization. Clients missed mortgage payments. The front office was fielding the same question on repeat. The back office was drowning in misdirected requests with no context. Nobody owned the handoff."

The Problem

Three teams. Zero coordination. Real consequences for clients.

The breakdown happened at the handoff between front office and back/middle office teams. When a client-facing associate identified a solution to an existing issue, there was no system to route it to the right resolution team. The process was informal: "who do you know in operations?" That question turned into an email - often to the wrong person - which then got forwarded, ignored, or lost.

What I Built

A TurboTax-style routing system. Built on the side. No budget.

Pathfinder is a SharePoint-backed ASPX workflow management system that routes client escalation cases to the correct resolution team based on a structured intake process. The front-end experience works like TurboTax - a client-facing associate opens a case, answers a series of questions about the nature of the problem, and the system narrows down which back or middle office team should own the resolution.

The right team gets a routed notification with full context. No more "who do you know in operations." No more emails to the wrong person. Every case is tracked, timestamped, and visible.

The Build

Six months to production. Six years in operation.

The first version took about six months to get right. We started small - two back office teams, 20 people on the resolution side, 60 on the front end submitting cases. We worked out the routing logic, refined the intake questions, and fixed what broke.

Buy-in came from senior managing directors across front, middle, and back office. It wasn't hard to get - the pain was visible and the solution was obvious. Nobody said no for long.

What I Learned

The best process improvements don't come from project charters.

Pathfinder wasn't funded. It wasn't assigned. Nobody asked me to build it. I built it because I understood the problem at ground level - I'd lived it as a financial consultant - and I had the initiative to do something about it when I got the chance.

That's the thing about process improvement: the people closest to the problem usually know the solution. The gap is almost never insight. It's ownership. Someone has to decide the problem is worth solving and go do it.

Six years later, 1,000+ people use something I built on the side with no budget. That's the version of program management I believe in.
